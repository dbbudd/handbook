The Role of the HKIS Teacher

Tier 1 Instruction

The HKIS Shared Philosophy of Education is the foundational anchor for this handbook — it carries our mission, vision, core values, and shared commitments. The role of the HKIS teacher begins from that shared foundation.

Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS) at HKIS

MTSS is a school-wide, data-informed framework designed to ensure students receive the right support, at the right time, with a preventive and proactive emphasis on: high-quality instruction for all, use of data to inform decisions, ongoing progress monitoring, and structured collaboration between educators, families, and students.

  • Tier 1: inclusive core instruction and classroom practices for all students (including UDL and differentiation)
  • Tier 2: targeted, time-limited supports for specific needs
  • Tier 3: intensive, individualised supports for complex or persistent needs

Teachers own Tier 1 instruction. Every HKIS teacher is responsible for designing and delivering high-quality learning experiences that are accessible to all students through proactive planning and responsive teaching.

Division-specific referral pathways, meeting structures, and documentation tools are linked from the Student Services Handbook and divisional Student Support/MTSS SharePoint pages.

Learning Design

How do we design meaningful learning experiences at HKIS?

What does strong learning design look like at HKIS?

At HKIS, strong learning design is a mission-driven act. Our mission calls us to: "Dedicating our minds to inquiry, our hearts to compassion, and our lives to service and global understanding. An American-style education grounded in the Christian faith and respecting the spiritual lives of all."

In practice, this means we design learning that treats inquiry as a pedagogical stance; intentionally nurtures compassion through social-emotional learning and spirituality; and creates meaningful opportunities for service, global understanding, and responsible citizenship.

Our written, documented curriculum is standards-based and is built to support understanding. We intentionally align curriculum, instruction, and assessment so students can demonstrate understanding over time. Students demonstrate understanding when they:

  1. Build important knowledge and skills
  2. Make meaning of the big ideas connected to that learning
  3. Transfer their learning to new and authentic situations

To support this, we use Understanding by Design (UbD) and begin with clarity about what matters most (desired results), identify evidence of learning (assessments), and then plan the learning experiences that build toward those outcomes. The HKIS Unit Planner is our key tool for documenting our written curriculum and translates our mission commitment into practice.

How do we use UbD to plan learning (Stages 1–3)?

We use UbD as a planning framework to ensure we teach for understanding, not coverage of a checklist.

  • Stage 1 (Desired Results) — Identify the prioritised standards and clarify the unit's anchor learning, especially the enduring understandings and essential questions.
  • Stage 2 (Evidence) — Plan for multiple ways students will show understanding. Formative assessment evidence guides instruction; summative tasks and success criteria are made clear to students.
  • Stage 3 (Learning Plan) — Design and sequence learning experiences that explicitly teach the knowledge and skills students need, continually returning to the unit's essential questions and enduring understandings.
How do standards and Reporting Taxonomies work together?

At HKIS, subject-specific Reporting Taxonomies articulate the most important learning we expect students to demonstrate over time. You can think about these as our prioritised standards framework. These taxonomies provide a coherent, guaranteed, and viable curriculum by establishing shared expectations for learning across classrooms, grade levels, and divisions.

Reporting Taxonomies are informed by discipline-specific standards frameworks (e.g., NGSS, C3, Common Core Math, National Core Arts Standards, ACTFL), which serve as anchor references for what students should know, understand, and be able to do. While these frameworks guide our work, they do not represent the full scope of the HKIS program.

We do not treat standards as a script or a checklist to "cover." Instead, they are used as reference points for designing learning that emphasises conceptual understanding and long-term transfer. Because many commonly used frameworks originate in the United States, part of our professional responsibility is to thoughtfully contextualise them for our Hong Kong and international community.

To ensure a guaranteed and viable curriculum, teaching teams collaborate closely with Learning Leaders and the Office for Teaching & Learning to prioritise the most important learning — aligned to our learning principles, focused on transfer, and realistic for students to achieve in our context. This collaborative work results in clear, shared Reporting Taxonomies for each subject area.

Each course/subject uses the subject-specific Reporting Taxonomy as its anchor reference.

What goes into each UbD stage (and how do standards fit)?

Stage 1 — Desired Results

  • Start from the agreed Reporting Taxonomy for the course/subject
  • Prioritise the standards so the unit is guaranteed and viable
  • Name the transfer goals: DTGs and TTGs where they authentically fit
  • Clarify meaning-making through enduring understandings and essential questions

Stage 2 — Evidence

  • Design assessments aligned to Stage 1
  • Use multiple measures: formative and summative evidence
  • Prioritise meaningful demonstrations of understanding, including authentic/performance-based tasks

Stage 3 — Learning Plan

  • Plan and sequence learning experiences that build toward Stage 1 outcomes
  • Keep the "thread" visible: return students to essential questions throughout
  • Design for learner variability: include scaffolds, differentiation, and UDL moves

We provide a coherent, guaranteed and viable curriculum through shared outcomes and common documentation. Within that, teachers have professional discretion in how they enact the learning plan — lesson design, sequencing, resources, scaffolds, inquiry approaches, and UDL/differentiation — while maintaining alignment to the shared intent in Stages 1–2.

How does UDL support planning for understanding and transfer?

UDL supports planning by helping teachers design learning that anticipates learner variability and removes barriers before they show up. Teachers keep the learning goals and success criteria stable, then proactively anticipate where a lesson might create barriers (e.g., language load, executive functioning demands, background knowledge, sensory/attention needs) and redesign for access, options, and agency.

  • Access — Students can find, understand, and use materials without unnecessary barriers
  • Options — Students have more than one way to engage with learning
  • Agency — Students make purposeful choices in service of the learning goal, with reflection

At HKIS, UDL is a proactive design stance within Tier 1 instruction. It is not "extra," and accessibility is not the same as an accommodation or modification.

How does service learning show up in our written curriculum?

At HKIS, service learning is not an add-on; it is intentionally planned in our written curriculum as one way we live our mission.

Service Learning Philosophy

Through service learning at HKIS, students develop an emotionally engaged and informed understanding of their immediate community and the world, the self-efficacy to make a positive impact in the lives of others, and a commitment to an ongoing journey of observation, investigation, action and reflection for the purpose of benefiting society and our natural environment.

Guiding Principles

We seek to empower students with the concepts, knowledge, skills, and dispositions to understand the challenges facing an interdependent world. Through service learning, students gain a deeper understanding of self, community, and society. We believe:

  • Students learn from, and are enriched by, the perspectives of others.
  • We have a responsibility to the community and natural environment in which we live.
  • Meaningful service is best achieved through sustained reciprocal partnerships.
  • Service has deeper meaning and impact when integrated into the curriculum.
  • Authentic and meaningful service is achieved through the five stages of service learning: Investigation, Preparation and Planning, Action, Reflection, and Demonstration.
  • Leadership, teamwork, communication, and mutual respect are fundamental for successful service-learning initiatives.

Common Agreements

We agree that service learning thrives when we are intentional about providing curricular, co-curricular, and extra-curricular opportunities for students to:

  • Engage in thoughtful observation, investigation, and planning;
  • Collaborate with others to achieve a shared goal;
  • Engage in regular purposeful reflection;
  • Integrate diverse perspectives;
  • Commit to and nurture multi-year partnerships;
  • Develop leadership and teamwork skills;
  • Value the question as much as the answer, process as much as the product, connections as much as the content;
  • Understand the larger sociocultural, environmental, and political factors of their actions and decisions.

Service Learning Standards

HKIS's service learning practices are guided by the Service Learning Standards (National Youth Leadership Council; Cathryn Berger Kaye):

  • Meaningful Service — actively engages participants in meaningful and personally relevant service.
  • Link to Curriculum — service-learning is intentionally used as an instructional strategy to meet learning goals/content standards.
  • Reflection — incorporates multiple challenging reflection activities.
  • Diversity — promotes understanding of diversity and mutual respect.
  • Youth Voice — students have a strong voice in planning, implementing, and evaluating.
  • Partnerships — collaborative, mutually beneficial, address community needs.
  • Progress Monitoring — ongoing assessment of quality and progress toward goals.
  • Duration and Intensity — sufficient duration and intensity to address community needs.

Instructional Practice

What does powerful teaching look like in HKIS classrooms? (Taught curriculum)

What are the HKIS Learning Principles and Practices?

Our Learning Principles and Practices describe the shared conditions and teacher practices we aim for across HKIS classrooms.

  1. Learning is nurtured in a safe and supportive environment. Therefore, we will:
    • build positive relationships;
    • seek ways to foster students' enjoyment of learning;
    • build strong learning communities that promote a culture of respect;
    • promote students' self-confidence, ability to see other perspectives, and willingness to take risks with their learning;
    • recognise mistakes as learning opportunities; and
    • provide space and resources conducive to learning.
  2. Learning is purposeful for life beyond school. Therefore, we will:
    • connect with, challenge, and extend students' present knowledge, skills, and understanding;
    • help students build skills in inquiry: asking probing questions and maintaining curiosity;
    • seek to connect students' learning and the world at large; and
    • focus on teaching for enduring understanding guided by essential questions.
  3. Learning results in the transfer of knowledge and skills to a variety of contexts. Therefore, we will:
    • provide opportunities for students to raise questions, solve problems, and reason;
    • use instruction and assessment strategies that support the transfer of learning; and
    • provide opportunities for students to demonstrate understanding by applying prior knowledge and skills to new challenges and experiences.
  4. Learning is personalised. Therefore, we will:
    • use a range of strategies to identify and respond to students' different learning and social needs, and cultural perspectives;
    • provide a variety of learning opportunities which enable students to build on personal strengths and interests; and
    • empower students to advocate for their own learning as appropriate.
  5. Learning is generated through interactions and integrated by the individual learner. Therefore, we will:
    • plan experiences that build and develop skills for learning through both collaboration and individual reflection; and
    • structure opportunities for students to learn with others inside and outside of the classroom.
  6. Learning is enhanced by student engagement in metacognition. Therefore, we will:
    • explicitly teach and model how to self-reflect, self-assess, and use feedback; and
    • empower students to communicate about their own process of learning.
  7. Learning is informed by effective assessments that address clear expectations. Therefore, we will:
    • share and construct learning goals with students;
    • use formative and summative assessment as an ongoing part of the teaching and learning process;
    • assess understanding in various ways, including authentic, performance-based tasks; and
    • use assessment data for teachers and students to guide future instruction and learning.
How do we structure lessons to support all learners (UDL)?

We structure lessons so that every student can access the learning, experience appropriate challenge, and stay meaningfully engaged. Teachers own Tier 1 instruction, using UDL to plan high-quality learning designed for learner variability.

  • Clarify the goal (the destination): What do you want students to leave understanding or able to do? Keep the goal separate from the format of the task/product.
  • Define success evidence: What will count as evidence of learning? When appropriate, allow more than one way for students to demonstrate success while still measuring the same goal.
  • Anticipate barriers (in the design, not the learner): Where might students get stuck because of the materials, methods, language/symbols, pacing, cognitive load, or environment?
  • Plan intentionally for access, options, and agency: Access (remove "can't get in" barriers); Options (multiple pathways toward the goal); Agency (purposeful choices aligned to the goal, with reflection).
  • Check for challenge and engagement, then refine: Use evidence from the lesson (student work, observation, exit prompts) to adjust supports, grouping, scaffolds, and opportunities for extension.

Tier 1 instruction is designed for learner variability. Teachers plan proactively to remove barriers so all learners can access rigorous learning goals.

How does HKIS approach inquiry, and what is my role as a teacher?

At HKIS, inquiry is not just a strategy — it is a stance. Our mission begins with "Dedicating our minds to inquiry," so having an inquiry stance is essential to understanding what powerful teaching looks like in HKIS classrooms. The HKIS Inquiry Framework provides a shared philosophy and common language for designing and enacting inquiry from Reception 1 through Grade 12, including the (non-linear) inquiry cycle of Engage, Explore, Apply, Reflect, and Connect, alongside the dispositions of Creativity, Collaboration, and Resilience.

In practice, your role is to design the conditions for inquiry and gradually move students into the driver's seat: make the learning goals visible; build curiosity through a purposeful provocation (e.g., phenomenon, issue, challenge); teach and scaffold the knowledge/skills students need to explore well; and keep reflection and connection active throughout (linking back to enduring understandings/essential questions and to students' lives, community, and the world).

This connects directly to UDL and student agency: we anticipate barriers and plan for learner variability so students have access to the inquiry, meaningful options for how they learn and show understanding, and authentic agency (voice, choice, and responsibility) aligned to clear and rigorous goals.

Powerful teaching includes an inquiry stance. Teachers are expected to deliver powerful teaching that supports students to dedicate their minds to inquiry by using the HKIS Inquiry Framework in their approaches and practice.

What information can I find out about my students?

At HKIS, a teacher's role is not simply to "cover" the written curriculum; it is to teach the learners you have. This means we actively seek to know our students — academically, socially, and emotionally — so that we can deliver Tier 1 instruction that is responsive, equitable, and aligned to rigorous goals.

  • Academic universal screeners and assessments (used to understand current performance, growth over time, and patterns of need): MAP Growth, DIBELS, MAP Fluency, STAMP, and other division/discipline assessments. These data help teachers plan appropriate challenge, group flexibly, target small skill gaps, and monitor whether instruction and supports are working.
  • Social-emotional / wellbeing universal screeners (used to understand strengths and needs that impact learning, belonging, and engagement). These data help teachers create the conditions for learning and to partner early with counselors and student support teams when patterns suggest additional follow-up.
  • Everyday classroom evidence: observation, conferencing, student work, formative checks, and student reflection. Universal screeners provide an important starting point; day-to-day evidence helps teachers interpret the "why" behind the data and respond in real time.

In an MTSS framework, these information sources support strong Tier 1 instruction and help teams make timely, student-centered decisions about scaffolds, extensions, and (when needed) targeted Tier 2/3 supports.

Schoolwide, the TRIPOD survey is administered biannually in UP, MS, and HS and provides students' perceptions of key teaching practices. TRIPOD results can be used for reflection, goal setting, and coaching conversations, helping teachers strengthen practice in ways that students experience directly.

What instructional approaches support conceptual understanding and transfer?

HKIS uses Understanding by Design (UbD) — so our instructional approaches must intentionally build students' conceptual understanding (meaning-making) and their ability to transfer learning independently to new and authentic situations. In UbD terms, teaching is not coverage or a sequence of activities; teachers act as coaches of understanding who keep the unit's enduring understandings, transfer goals, and essential questions active as a thread through daily lessons.

In Stage 3 (Learning Plan), we therefore design learning experiences that support acquisition (knowledge/skills), meaning making (making sense of big ideas), and transfer (applying learning to solve problems, reason, and perform with understanding), using UDL/differentiation so all learners can access rigorous goals.

How do we adapt instruction in response to evidence of student learning?

HKIS positions assessment as an ongoing process embedded within the cycles of teaching and learning, used to guide future instruction and learning. We emphasise collecting multiple forms of evidence (formative and summative) and using assessment data for teachers and students to guide future instruction and learning.

What teaching and learning resources are available, and how do teachers access them?

HKIS provides shared digital spaces in Microsoft Teams to support collaboration and access to curriculum resources, including the SW Curriculum Channel. The SW Curriculum Channel is the source of truth for verified written curriculum and course/unit resources.

Additionally, divisions are equipped with Coaching Offices or Teaching and Learning Centres and have support available from curriculum leaders, coaches, and teacher leaders.

Assessment, Grading, Feedback & Reporting

How do we know students are learning?

What is our Philosophy of Assessment?

Assessment, Grading and Reporting

Assessment, grading and reporting are elements of a feedback cycle that supports learning.

  • Assessment is the ongoing process of gathering a variety of evidence of student learning, embedded within the cycles of teaching and learning. Evidence may be collected through observation, conversation, and reviews of student work and performances.
  • Grading is the process of evaluating student evidence against a set of standards/criteria in order to provide feedback to students about their progress toward the intended learning.
  • Reporting is the communication of learning progress, growth, and achievement to students, parents and the wider community.

At HKIS we believe

The purpose of an effective assessment, grading and reporting system is to clearly, accurately, and consistently support student learning, by communicating learning progress, growth, and achievement to students, families, educators, and external institutions. This ensures all members have the information they need to make important decisions about a student's readiness for future opportunities.

Therefore, HKIS educators will:

  1. Measure, document and report student progress and proficiency against a set of clearly defined content-area standards and learning objectives.
  2. Collaborate and calibrate with colleagues to ensure aligned grading practices.
  3. Measure and provide feedback on behaviours and dispositions separately from academic progress.
  4. Provide timely, relevant, and actionable feedback about student growth and areas of strength in a manner that empowers and motivates students.
  5. Provide students with opportunities for self evaluation and reflection on their strengths, areas for growth, and next steps.
  6. Use data from grading and reporting to make informed decisions about educational programs, in order to support each student's learning journey.
What do we believe about effective assessment and grading at HKIS?

At HKIS, assessment is the ongoing process of gathering a variety of evidence of student learning, embedded within the cycle of teaching and learning. Grading is the process of evaluating evidence against a set of criteria in order to provide feedback to students about their progress towards the intended learning.

Our assessment practices are guided by four shared principles and practices:

  1. Assessment Measures What We Value
  2. Assessment Serves Learning
  3. Multiple Measures Provide Clear Evidence of Learning
  4. Assessments Are Equitable

Assessment includes both formative and summative processes. Formative assessment provides ongoing feedback to guide next steps in learning, while summative assessment provides a judgment of performance at a point in time. Together, they contribute to a comprehensive picture of student understanding.

How do we design assessments that measure understanding and support transfer?

Assessment is planned through UbD Stage 2 (Evidence) and aligned to clearly defined learning goals and standards. At HKIS, we prioritise:

  • Authentic, performance-based tasks
  • Opportunities for students to apply learning in meaningful contexts
  • Alignment between learning goals, instructional experiences, and assessment methods

Effective assessments provide evidence of:

  • Acquisition of knowledge and skills
  • Meaning-making and conceptual understanding
  • Transfer of learning to new and authentic situations
How do we use feedback to empower students as learners?

Feedback at HKIS is a collaborative communication process that advances learning and empowers students.

Effective feedback:

  • Is aligned to clear learning goals
  • Is timely, specific, and actionable
  • Is separate from grades
  • Engages students in reflection, goal-setting, and next steps

Students are provided opportunities to act on feedback through revision, practice, and reassessment, supporting the development of self-regulated learners.

How do we report?

Reporting is the communication of learning progress, growth, and achievement to students, guardians and the wider community.

The HKIS Reporting System communicates student learning progress, growth and achievement. It allows us to recognise students for all their attributes, capabilities, passions, achievements, and interests with the goal of providing a complete picture of each student.

Reporting at HKIS is a multifaceted system that encompasses many elements, including but not limited to:

  • Gradebook (Schoology)
  • SeeSaw
  • Student-parent conferences
  • Portfolios
  • Report card (PowerSchool)

The Reporting System gives all students, educators, and families equitable access to the learning data and ensures all stakeholders have the information they need to make important decisions about a student's readiness for future opportunities.

How is the reporting taxonomy used for assessing, grading, reporting, and feedback purposes?

The HKIS Reporting Taxonomy provides a common language for how learning outcomes are organised and communicated across subjects (because standards frameworks use different organisational terms). The taxonomy includes:

  • Reporting Categories (collections of measurement topics used on summary reports)
  • Measurement Topics (groups of related learning goals that inform unit and assessment design)
  • Learning Goals (specific performance outcomes derived from grade-level outcomes)
  • Learning Targets (lesson-sized progressions toward learning goals)

It connects assessment, feedback, grading, and reporting by ensuring that evidence of learning is interpreted consistently and tied to our most important learning outcomes.

At HKIS, teachers use the Reporting Taxonomy to:

  • Identify what evidence of learning is collected (Measurement Topics within Reporting Categories),
  • Interpret patterns of evidence to determine levels of proficiency using the General Academic Scale, and
  • Communicate learning clearly to students and families through feedback, conferences, and report cards.

While the taxonomy structure is shared schoolwide, the specific practices for assessment, grading, reporting, and reassessment are defined at the divisional level to ensure developmental appropriateness and programme alignment.

Learning Environment & Student Wellbeing

How do we create classrooms where every student can thrive?

What does belonging look like in HKIS classrooms?

We frame belonging as an essential condition for growth, well-being, and success. We commit to fostering a community where people feel valued, respected, and empowered, and we emphasise mutual respect, inclusion, and support. Belonging is the emotional and experiential outcome of inclusion.

How do we support students' social and emotional development (SEL) within daily instruction?

At HKIS, social-emotional learning (SEL) is best understood as bigger than Advisory/PCG or a standalone lesson. SEL is built through the daily interactions, routines, and learning experiences that help students (and adults) develop healthy identities, manage emotions, reach goals, show empathy, build relationships, and make responsible decisions. In our MTSS framework, SEL is a Tier 1 universal support: at least 80% of students should have their social-emotional needs met through inclusive classroom practices. Schoolwide, our SEL approach is aligned to CASEL's five core competencies, and is supported through division-specific programming and counseling services.

  • Design for psychological safety and belonging: establish predictable routines, clear expectations, and a classroom culture grounded in dignity, inclusion, and respect.
  • Teach the learning behaviours students need to engage: explicitly model and practise skills such as self-management, collaboration, reflection, and help-seeking/self-advocacy.
  • Use relationship-centered practices: build connection through regular check-ins, noticing, restorative language, and feedback that supports growth.
  • Build voice, choice, and agency: create meaningful opportunities for student agency aligned to learning goals (not just choice for its own sake).
  • Use data and team support early: notice patterns (not one-off moments) and partner with counselors/learning support and the Student Review Team process when additional targeted supports may be needed.

Division-specific SEL structures, tools, and referral processes vary by division and should be accessed through the HKIS Student Services Handbook and the divisional Student Support/MTSS SharePoint pages (source of truth).

As we continue building coherence across the school, HKIS is working toward shared Tier 1 SEL language using CASEL competencies, supported by division-specific programming. Teams may also use vertical story progressions ("stories before standards") as a developmental narrative to help prioritise SEL skills, routines, and expectations at different stages.

What is HKIS's schoolwide approach to health & wellbeing, and what does it mean for classroom practice?

At HKIS, we aim to engage in a culture that actively supports the social, emotional, environmental, physical, mental, and spiritual wellbeing of all members of our school community. We understand that learning happens best when all members of the community feel healthy, safe, supported and engaged. To frame this work, we implement the Whole School, Whole Community, Whole Child (WSCC) approach, which helps us coordinate the components that promote health and wellbeing. (Schoolwide Health & Wellbeing Philosophy, ratified May 28, 2025)

The WSCC components at HKIS include:

  • Physical education and physical activity
  • Nutrition environment and services
  • Health education
  • Social and emotional climate
  • Physical environment
  • Health services
  • Counseling, psychological and social services
  • Employee wellness
  • Community involvement
  • Family engagement
  • Spiritual wellbeing (HKIS mission specific)

For teachers, this philosophy shows up in daily classroom practice through the intentional design of learning environments and routines that promote safety, belonging, and engagement; learning experiences that support students' capacity to manage emotions, collaborate, and persist; and proactive partnership with the wider support system when student needs extend beyond what Tier 1 classroom supports can provide.

How do we shape classroom cultures that support curiosity, belonging, and learning?

We shape classroom cultures by centering learning in safe, supportive, and respectful environments and intentionally building relationships and strong learning communities. This includes fostering belonging (valued, respected, empowered), encouraging intellectual risk-taking and curiosity (including inquiry approaches), and using collaborative, actionable feedback practices that support student growth.

Common professional learning practices at HKIS

  • Learning Walks
    • What: A non-evaluative inquiry process where small groups of educators visit classrooms to notice and reflect on the learning environment, student learning, and instructional strategies.
    • Why: To observe practice in action, learn through dialogue, and surface ideas that strengthen student learning.
    • How: Set a focus question; visit classrooms in small groups and take non-evaluative notes; debrief and reflect; the facilitator shares a brief summary of noticings and appreciations with the host educator(s).
  • Media Study
    • What: A shared inquiry process that builds professional knowledge through dialogue around a topic using media (e.g., articles, books, podcasts, lectures).
    • Why: To connect research and professional dialogue to classroom practice and student learning needs.
    • How: A facilitator curates resources and invites participants; the group sets a schedule and shared facilitation roles; the group identifies next steps and (when appropriate) shares learning with the wider faculty.
  • Lab Sites
    • What: Short-term, collaborative opportunities where teachers and coaches design, test, and reflect on instructional strategies (one-time or ongoing; internally or externally facilitated).
    • Why: To build instructional capacity through observation, rehearsal, and feedback focused on student learning.
    • How: Identify a focus; co-plan and assign roles; model or co-teach (recording if appropriate); try strategies in classrooms; debrief, reflect, and refine next steps.
  • Coaching Cycles
    • What: An iterative, collaborative process between an educator and a coach, grounded in a goal to improve student learning.
    • Why: Sustained instructional growth is supported when educators work side-by-side over time with a shared focus on student outcomes.
    • How: Identify a student-centered goal informed by standards and evidence; select instructional moves and a plan; implement; analyse evidence and reflect; determine next steps.
  • Workshop Models
    • What: Facilitated learning sessions that help educators deepen understanding of frameworks, practices, or skills (e.g., workshops, micro-PDs, Teachers Teaching Teachers).
    • Why: To strengthen shared practice, grow new approaches, and respond to evolving student needs through collaborative learning.
    • How: Participate in an extended workshop, a brief optional micro-PD, or a Teachers Teaching Teachers session where colleagues share expertise in a supportive, low-risk environment.
  • Curriculum Development
    • What: Collaborative curriculum planning with educators and coaches/curriculum leaders using backward design to strengthen units and course learning.
    • Why: Coherent, aligned unit development and implementation supports optimal student learning.
    • How: Review current unit plans; plan the work and decision points; revise units collaboratively; support unit launches with coaches/curriculum leaders; identify the instructional calendar progression for the following year.
  • Meeting Facilitation
    • What: Purposeful facilitation using structures and protocols to help teams collaborate around curriculum, instruction, assessment, and shared problems of practice.
    • Why: To build shared understanding and alignment, strengthening collective practice and student outcomes.
    • How: Clarify outcomes; design an agenda and select protocols; facilitate dialogue and decision-making; reflect on impact and determine follow-up support as needed.
What is Self-Directed Professional Growth (SDPG)?

Self-Directed Professional Growth (SDPG) (Performance Appraisal) defines how HKIS teachers pursue professional goals during SDPG years. SDPG options share a common cycle of: initial reflection (including self-assessment using the relevant professional practice rubric and, for classroom teachers, reflection on TRIPOD student feedback), goal setting (connected to student learning and tied to one to two rubric areas), self-directed practice improvement, peer and/or administrator feedback, and a final reflection and sharing of learning.

  • Coaching cycles with an instructional coach centered on an educator's professional goal.
  • Collaborative problems of practice with peers (pairs, teams, PLCs) who co-develop a shared goal and use evidence/artifacts and peer feedback protocols.
  • Video-based self-assessment using a filmed lesson/session, followed by analysis and a learning-focused conversation with a peer.
  • Action research (plan–do–check–act cycles) to test a strategy connected to a problem of practice, collect evidence, and refine next steps.
  • Learning-walk engagement with structured observation, debrief, and reflection on strategies to try and follow up on.
  • Administrator walkthroughs with a minimum series of walkthroughs and actionable feedback aligned to the educator's goal.
  • Other (educator proposed) options, discussed and agreed with an administrator at the beginning of the year.

Students will develop the skills they need to form genuine relationships in our diverse society and to make contributions to our community.

  • Working well with others and respecting other points of view
  • Accepting diversity and building cross-cultural relationships
  • Actively participating, listening, and making connections with others
  • Working with others to achieve goals and helping to resolve conflicts

Students will make contributions by:

  • Identifying a need and voluntarily participating in service
  • Contributing time, talent, or money, often involving personal sacrifice
  • Following through on commitments and reflecting on service experiences
  • Studying in the classroom issues raised by their service experiences

Students will exhibit global citizenship by:

  • Remaining conversant on current events and aware of global impacts
  • Confronting discrimination and stereotypes
  • Developing a multicultural perspective and empathising with those representing a minority position
What does it mean to learn to serve and serve to learn?

We believe:

  1. Students learn from, and are enriched by, the perspectives of others
  2. We have a responsibility to the community and natural environment in which we live
  3. Meaningful service is best achieved through sustained reciprocal partnerships
  4. Service has deeper meaning and impact when integrated into the curriculum
  5. Authentic service is achieved through the five stages: Investigation, Preparation & Planning, Action, Reflection, and Demonstration
  6. Leadership, teamwork, communication, and mutual respect are fundamental

Tight (Required)

We agree that service learning thrives when we are intentional about providing opportunities for students to: engage in thoughtful investigation and planning; collaborate to achieve shared goals; engage in regular purposeful reflection; commit to and nurture multi-year partnerships; and understand the larger sociocultural, environmental, and political factors of their actions.

How do we ensure service learning is authentic and sustained?

HKIS's service learning practices are guided by the National Youth Leadership Council standards:

  1. Meaningful Service — personally relevant activities that actively engage participants
  2. Link to Curriculum — intentionally used as an instructional strategy to meet learning goals
  3. Reflection — multiple, ongoing reflection activities that prompt deep thinking
  4. Diversity — promotes understanding of diversity and mutual respect
  5. Youth Voice — students have a strong voice in planning, implementing, and evaluating
  6. Partnerships — collaborative, mutually beneficial, and address community needs
  7. Progress Monitoring — ongoing assessment of quality and progress toward goals
  8. Duration and Intensity — sufficient to address needs and meet outcomes

Spirituality

Grounded in the Christian faith and respecting the spiritual lives of all.

HKIS is grounded in the Christian faith and is respectful of the spiritual lives of all. The Christian faith is a response to the redemptive grace and love found in Jesus Christ, and recognises that each student is a precious child of God. Spirituality at HKIS is a journey of reverence that explores the meaning of our lives and connections with God, other people, and the world.

SLR: Spirituality

Students will understand and respect Christianity and other religions and will identify and develop their own spiritual identity.

Students will exhibit understanding of Christianity by:

  • Describing and explaining the key principles of Christianity
  • Connecting Christian terminology and symbols to key principles
  • Making sense of the multiple ways the Bible is interpreted within the Christian community
  • Comparing key concepts, teachings, practices, and texts of Christianity
  • Recognising a connection between Christian principles and ethical conduct

Students will exhibit understanding of other religions by:

  • Describing and explaining the key principles of other religions
  • Connecting terminology and symbols from other religions to key principles
  • Making sense of the multiple ways other sacred writings are interpreted
  • Comparing key concepts, teachings, practices, and texts of other religions
  • Recognising connections between the principles of other religions and ethical conduct

Students will demonstrate respect for Christianity and other religions by:

  • Respecting the religious rituals/spirituality of others
  • Respecting the diverse religious and spiritual lives and traditions of others

Students will demonstrate their personal spiritual identities by:

  • Engaging in dialogue about Christianity and other religious worldviews in daily life
  • Identifying connections with their personal beliefs and others
  • Reflecting upon their own spiritual identities
  • Living out their worldviews in a variety of ways
How does HKIS nurture spiritual development across the school?

Spiritual development is woven into the fabric of school life — through Religious Education classes, chapels, community gatherings, and co-curricular opportunities across all divisions:

  • Lower & Upper Primary: Classroom teachers teach Religious Education, covering Christianity and world religions. Students may join JOY Club and Bible Study Fellowship.
  • Middle School: Dedicated RE teachers cover Old Testament (G6), New Testament and world religions (G7-8). Students can join GLOW Club and Worship on Wednesdays.
  • High School: Biblical and world religion courses are integrated into humanities and fine arts. Students complete a Spiritual Exploration (SPEX) course and one RE elective. FISH Club and Worship on Fridays are available.

Community is nurtured through weekly parent prayer groups, family worship events, and the annual GIFTS Conference.

What does spiritual development look like for HKIS graduates?

HKIS graduates will:

  1. Recognise, define, and articulate their own spiritual journey and how it guides their life
  2. Articulate why Jesus is central to the biblical narrative and Christianity, and why Jesus may be important to their own or other people's lives
  3. Engage respectfully with various religious and spiritual perspectives

Technology

Empowering students to leverage digital tools to drive their own learning — responsibly, ethically, and creatively.

At HKIS, technology is not a separate subject — it is integrated across all divisions and disciplines. Guided by the Washington State K-12 Educational Technology Standards (ISTE-informed), students develop as Empowered Learners who leverage technology to take an active role in choosing, achieving, and demonstrating competency in their learning goals. This connects directly to one of our six Student Learning Results.

SLR: Self-Motivated Learning

Students willingly apply a variety of learning and motivation strategies throughout their learning process.

Students demonstrate persistence and engagement by:

  • Recognising their intellectual style, strengths, and weaknesses
  • Consistently applying different strategies to enhance interest until a goal is achieved
  • Sustaining or increasing effort in the face of difficulty
  • Actively seeking help when necessary

Students exhibit risk-taking by:

  • Applying new strategies to different assignments and projects
  • Seeking and engaging in challenging tasks that enhance learning
  • Exploring new interests

Students demonstrate application of a variety of learning and motivation strategies by:

  • Engaging in effective thinking about their own thinking (metacognition)
  • Knowing a variety of key learning and motivation strategies
  • Appropriately applying a variety of learning and motivation strategies
  • Respecting the diverse learning needs and styles of other students

Students demonstrate control and regulation of their learning process by:

  • Accurately self-assessing on an ongoing basis
  • Continuously reflecting, setting, monitoring, and revising goals
  • Accepting feedback and criticism without defensiveness
  • Adapting and adjusting learning and motivation strategies to meet their needs

Students enjoy the process of learning by:

  • Pursuing personal interests
  • Taking learning risks when their interest is piqued
  • Showing persistence in an area of passion

Technology connection: The Washington State standards lead with Empowered Learner — a student who leverages technology to take an active role in their learning goals. When students use technology to self-assess, seek resources, explore new interests, and adapt strategies, they are living out Self-Motivated Learning in a digital context.

How do students develop as empowered, self-directed users of technology?

The Washington State standards define seven competencies that develop progressively across divisions:

Lower Primary (R1-G2): Foundation for digital literacy and responsible online behaviour. Students are introduced as active participants and creators with strong educator guidance — building empowered learning, digital citizenship, knowledge construction, design thinking, computational skills, and creative communication.

Upper Primary (G3-G5): Deepening understanding and application. Students develop Digital Citizenship skills to navigate the online world safely and ethically, refining critical thinking, innovation, and computational thinking.

Middle School (G6-G8): Responsible and ethical digital navigation. The curriculum emphasises Critical Thinking, Innovative Design, and Digital Citizenship — evaluating online information and leveraging technology for creative problem-solving.

High School (G9-G12): Advanced technological skills, deep understanding of digital ethics, and the ability to critically evaluate information. Technology becomes integral to social interactions, access to information, and future career preparation.

How do we ensure technology is used responsibly, ethically, and with integrity?

Students sign a Technology Responsible Use Agreement each year. As faculty, we set the conditions for responsible use. Our role is to design when, how, and why technology is used in learning — including emerging tools like Generative AI.

Tight (Required)

Faculty are expected to:

  • Communicate expectations clearly — device and tool use expectations at the task, unit, or course level
  • Design technology use intentionally — technology should enhance learning, creativity, or connection; not be used for its own sake
  • Model balanced use — prioritise in-person interaction, movement, rest, and reflection alongside digital tools
  • Apply consistent integrity standards — the same expectations for honesty and attribution apply to AI-supported work as to all other tools
  • Use only approved tools — all software and digital tools used with students must go through the HKIS Software Selection process, which includes a data privacy review

Not all screen time is equal. Productive, creative, and collaborative use differs from passive or distracting use. Help students develop the self-regulation to know the difference — this is a long-term learning process, not a short-term enforcement issue.

Artificial Intelligence at HKIS

The HKIS Roadmap for Artificial Intelligence is a strategic guide for integrating AI in ways that reflect our mission, uphold our values, and strengthen existing teaching and learning frameworks. Our approach is anchored in guiding questions adapted from Common Sense Media:

  • How does AI work, and how does it affect us?
  • How can I use AI responsibly?
  • How can AI help us learn better?

Tight (Required)

Faculty use only HKIS-approved AI tools with students when integrating AI into teaching and learning.

Glossary

Key terms used across HKIS curriculum documentation. Highlighted terms appear inline throughout the handbook — hover or tap to see definitions.

Adaptive Schools
A framework and professional development approach designed to build collaborative, flexible, and effective school communities that improve teaching and learning outcomes. HKIS integrates and uses Adaptive Schools approaches and protocols to facilitate effective meetings, structure professional learning, and engage in dialogue.
Assessment
The ongoing process of gathering a variety of evidence of student learning, embedded within the cycle of teaching and learning.
Belonging
The emotional and experiential outcome of inclusion — when people feel valued, respected, and empowered in a community.
Case Manager
A designated member of the Student Support Team who coordinates a student's support plan by clarifying goals, documenting accommodations/interventions, facilitating communication between school and home, monitoring progress using data, and leading regular plan reviews and updates. Case managers are typically assigned based on the primary area of need (counselor for social-emotional/behavioral; learning specialist for academic).
Coaching
Encompasses both the formal role of an Instructional Coach ("big C") and broader coaching conversations ("small c"). Both are grounded in evidence of student learning and are distinct from performance appraisal.
Curriculum (n. / v.)
Curriculum (n.) refers to the organised framework that guides teaching and learning within a school. Curriculum (v.) is the continuous process of designing, implementing, evaluating, and refining learning experiences to achieve educational goals.
Disciplinary Transfer Goals (DTGs)
Long-term "exit goals" developed from a discipline's standards framework and from the beliefs that underpin that framework. If teachers are addressing the standards, they are addressing the DTGs.
Feedback
A collaborative process of communication between members of a learning community that nurtures and empowers students in their individual learning journeys; embedded within cycles of teaching and learning and includes specific evidence in relation to identified learning goals.
General Academic Scale (GAS)
A common proficiency language used at HKIS to align assessment, reporting, and classroom practices: Emerging, Developing, Exhibiting, Exhibiting Depth.
Grading
The process of evaluating evidence of student learning against a set of criteria in order to provide feedback to students about their progress toward intended learning.
Guaranteed and Viable Curriculum
A coherent curriculum anchored in shared long-term ends. Guaranteed because teachers work collectively to develop and strengthen long-term goals and shared outcomes across grades/courses. Viable because teams keep long-term ends in mind and prioritise what is taught accordingly.
Inquiry (HKIS Inquiry Framework)
A shared, research-based philosophy and common language intended to be reflected in curriculum design and evident through instruction. Some inquiry skills may be explicitly outlined in subject standards; in those cases, inquiry outcomes sit in Stage 1 (Desired Results).
Learning Leaders
Members of the Teaching & Learning Team who work alongside teachers to strengthen curriculum, teaching, and learning across the school. They help ensure that what we teach, how we teach, and how we support students are aligned with our shared philosophy, schoolwide priorities, and current research. Learning Leaders have specific areas of responsibility as designated by subject area.
Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS)
A school-wide, data-informed framework designed to ensure students receive the right support, at the right time. MTSS emphasises high-quality Tier 1 instruction for all students, targeted Tier 2 supports when needed, and intensive Tier 3 supports for complex or persistent needs.
Performance Task
A learning activity or assessment that asks students to "perform" with their learning — construct a multifaceted response, create a product, or produce a demonstration (i.e., apply learning).
Reporting
The communication of learning progress, growth, and achievement to students, parents and the wider community.
Reporting Taxonomy
A common HKIS language for how outcomes are organised and communicated across subjects (because different standards frameworks use different organisational terms). Key components include Reporting Categories (used on summary reports), Measurement Topics (groups of related learning goals), Learning Goals (specific performance outcomes), and Learning Targets ("lesson-sized chunks" that build toward learning goals over time).
Social Emotional Learning (SEL)
The ongoing development of skills and mindsets that help students (and adults) build healthy identities, manage emotions, reach goals, show empathy, build relationships, and make responsible decisions — strengthened through daily routines and practices, not only through a standalone program.
Standards vs. Curriculum
Standards are broad benchmarks that guide the development of curricula; curriculum is what we design/teach/assess in our context to achieve educational goals.
Subject Area Leaders (Primary School)
Teachers responsible for supporting coherence and quality within a subject area (sometimes called department heads or similar roles). Subject area leaders commonly coordinate curriculum collaboration, support shared assessment and resource development, and serve as a liaison between teaching teams and school/divisional leadership.
Teacher Leaders
Educators who lead and support colleagues while remaining grounded in classroom practice. Teacher leaders typically facilitate team/department collaboration, help steward shared agreements and resources, and support implementation through coaching, modelling, and coordination.
Transdisciplinary Transfer Goals (TTGs)
Creativity, Collaboration, and Resilience — transfer goals that apply across learning areas.
Understanding by Design (UbD)
A planning framework used at HKIS with Stage 1 (Desired Results), Stage 2 (Evidence), and Stage 3 (Learning Plan).
Universal Design for Learning (UDL)
A proactive instructional design approach that anticipates learner variability and removes barriers before they show up. UDL supports access, offers options for engagement and learning pathways, and builds student agency — while keeping learning goals clear and rigorous.
